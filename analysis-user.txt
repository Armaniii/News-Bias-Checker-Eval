Types of media bias:- Spin: Vague or sensational language, including substituting an interpretive verb for a neutral one. Example: Writing "Senator X gloated over the bill's passage" rather than "said" or "responded" — the word 'gloated' implies smug pleasure without evidence, in the author's own voice.- Unsubstantiated claims: Claims without evidence.- Opinion Statements Presented as Fact: Subjective judgments presented as objective truth, without attribution. Example: A reporter writes in their own voice "The tax cuts failed to stimulate economic growth" — whether a policy "failed" is a contested  causal judgment that belongs in analysis, not unattributed news copy. - Sensationalism and Emotionalism: Information presented to shock.- Mudslinging/Ad Hominem: Unfair insults or attacks on character.- Mind reading: Assuming knowledge of others' thoughts.- Slant: Telling only part of the story present in the article — where information exists in the article but is structurally de-emphasized.   Example: An article reports a protester was injured prominently in the opening paragraphs, but mentions that three officers were also treated for injuries only in a single sentence near the end, attributed to a police statement. Both facts appear, but unequal prominence shapes the reader's impression. - Bias by Omission: Omitting information that would support an alternative viewpoint entirely — where the alternative perspective is absent from the article, not merely de-emphasized.   Example: An article on immigration enforcement quotes only affected families and advocacy organizations. No law enforcement perspective, border security rationale, or policy justification appears anywhere in the piece — the alternative viewpoint does not exist in the article at all. - Subjective Qualifying Adjectives: Bias revealed through adjectives.- Word Choice: Politically loaded words.- Negativity Bias: Emphasizing negative news.- Elite vs. Populist Bias: Deferring to prestigious institutions vs. dissenting perspectives. Exclude quotes from analysis. Be cautious and humble in your analysis. Present fewer, confident examples rather than more uncertain ones. CRITICAL ATTRIBUTION RULE: When the author reproduces a quoted source's statement at length (e.g., a full statement, a press release, an email), the language in that statement belongs ENTIRELY to the source, not the author. Do not flag language from extended reproduced statements as the author's bias, even when the language is extreme. The author's bias is revealed only in: (a) their own connecting prose, (b) their choice of which sources to include, (c) the framing of those sources, and (d) what they choose to omit.
