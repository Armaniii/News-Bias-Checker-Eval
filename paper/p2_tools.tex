\begin{abstract}
News-bias products---bias checkers, balanced-news feeds, media-literacy
tools---are increasingly built by asking a frontier model to flag biased
spans, label an article's lean, and explain itself. But which of those
outputs can a builder trust? We evaluate two frontier target models and
two cross-family judges on 200 expert-rated U.S.\ news articles and find
a sharp split. \emph{Trustworthy} primitives: the \emph{volume} of
flagged spans is a valid meter of an article's partisan intensity
(Spearman $\rho \approx 0.6$ vs.\ expert ratings), and cross-model
\emph{agreement} predicts correctness cleanly (two models agreeing on
lean are $74\%$ accurate vs.\ $22\%$ when they disagree; four agreeing,
$87\%$). \emph{Untrustworthy} primitives: a single model's lean label
near the center-right, its ``bias by omission'' or ``unsubstantiated
claim'' flags (chance-corrected agreement $\kap \le 0.14$), and the
specific text it highlights (cross-model overlap $0.26$) are
model-specific opinion, not detected fact. We also document a
directional failure mode shared by all four models---moderate-right
articles are read as center or \emph{left}, so a lean-labeling tool
built on them systematically under-counts right-leaning content---and
translate the map into deployable triage rules: an agreement-based
router clears $43\%$ of volume at $85\%$ accuracy while catching $86\%$
of errors. Findings are exploratory (not pre-registered) and inherit
AllSides outlet labels as ground truth.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
A growing class of products puts a frontier language model in the role
of a news-bias analyst: it highlights loaded spans, assigns a
left/right lean, summarizes ``both sides,'' and narrates why. The
implicit assumption is that these outputs are measurements. Many are
not---they are one model's opinion, and a different frontier model,
shown the same article, would say something else. A builder needs to
know, before shipping, \emph{which} outputs reproduce and which do not,
and how to triage the difference.

This paper supplies that reliability map. We do not ask whether the
models are biased (a companion paper takes up the behavioral question);
we ask which of a bias detector's outputs a tool can present to a reader
as reliable. The answer is a clean split between primitives anchored to
explicit text---which reproduce across independent models---and
primitives that require inference about what is absent, implied, or
where an article sits on a scale---which do not. We close with the
practical form of the map: routing rules, and one systematic,
directional error a builder must correct for.

\textbf{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{A reliability map of bias-detection primitives.} Which
  outputs of a frontier-model bias detector reproduce across models
  (detection volume; cross-model agreement) and which are
  model-specific opinion (lean labels near center-right; omission and
  framing flags; specific highlights)---each with a number.
  \item \textbf{A directional failure mode shared across vendors.} All
  four models read moderate-right content as center-or-left, so a
  lean-labeling tool built on them under-counts the right; we localize
  the hardest class, topics, and per-model detection habits.
  \item \textbf{Deployable triage rules.} Agreement-based routing and a
  primitive-by-primitive display policy that a product can adopt
  directly, plus a $\sim$\$150 per-model-upgrade regression audit.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Setup}
We reuse the corpus, models, and rollouts of the companion study; we
summarize here and defer the full protocol (including the reliability
gates behind the judge instruments) to it. The corpus is $N{=}200$
contemporary U.S.\ news articles on a balanced $5\times8$ grid: five
AllSides-anchored lean classes (Left, Lean~Left, Center, Lean~Right,
Right; 40 each) $\times$ eight topics, models receiving article text
only (no outlet or headline). Two frontier \emph{target} models
(Claude Sonnet~4.5, GPT-4.1) and two cross-family \emph{judge} models
(Claude Sonnet~4.6, GPT-5) perform three tasks: span-level bias
detection with a shared bias-type schema
\citep{spinde2021babe,hamborg2019automated}, summarization with
represented-perspective counts, and five-class lean classification with
confidence. All analyses below are exploratory and use expert AllSides
labels as ground truth \citep{baly2020detect}.

\section{Which primitives reproduce}

\textbf{Detection volume is a valid intensity meter.} The simplest
possible signal---how many spans a model flags---tracks how loaded an
article actually is: flagged-span count rises monotonically with the
article's expert-rated partisan intensity (Spearman $\rho{=}0.64$ for
Sonnet, $0.58$ for GPT-4.1 against $|\text{lean rating}|$, both
$p{<}10^{-18}$; partisan articles draw $2.3\times$ the flags of Center
articles). A tool can trust a flag \emph{count} as a bias meter even
where it cannot trust the individual flags.

\textbf{Cross-model agreement predicts correctness.} The strongest cheap
reliability signal is two independent models agreeing. On five-class
lean, when the two target models agree they are $74\%$ accurate; when
they disagree, only $22\%$ (n${=}199$ shared articles; they agree
$61\%$ of the time). Adding the two judges sharpens it into a ladder:
when all four models agree ($44\%$ of articles) the modal label is
$87\%$ accurate, falling monotonically to $75\%$ at three-of-four and
$62\%$ at two (Table~\ref{tab:consensus}). Agreement is a free,
ground-truth-independent confidence signal.

\textbf{Only concrete, word-anchored bias forms reproduce.} Given both
target models the same bias-type schema, we ask how often they agree a
given form is present in an article (Cohen's $\kap$ over 256
jointly-rated articles). No form clears moderate agreement---the ceiling
is opinion-stated-as-fact at $\kap{=}0.45$---and the forms defined by
what is \emph{absent from} or \emph{unverified against} the text
collapse toward chance (Table~\ref{tab:biastype}). The reliable end is
anchored to explicit words present in the text; the unreliable end
requires reasoning beyond the page. \emph{The disagreement is not
random}: the two models have distinct detection habits---Claude leads
with bias-by-omission ($14\%$ of its flags), GPT-4.1 with sensationalism
($15\%$)---so one hunts what a story omits while the other hunts its
tone, which is exactly why they cannot agree omission is present. The
unreliability reaches the span level too: even when both models flag the
same article, the specific text they highlight overlaps at only Jaccard
$0.26$. For a tool: show the concrete word-level flags, and treat any
single model's omission, framing, or unsupported-claim flag---and any
individual highlight---as one model's opinion, surfaced only on
cross-model agreement.

\textbf{A cheap word-list is a corpus monitor, not a per-item detector.}
A deterministic paired left/right lexicon corroborates aggregate
directional patterns, but as a filter for a judge's per-output verdict
it reaches only precision $0.25$, recall $0.30$---so it belongs in a
corpus-level regression check, never as a live per-article flag.

\section{The directional failure mode}

The reproducible signals above are politically even-handed on average.
The classification \emph{errors} are not. Breaking accuracy down by true
class exposes a single hard case shared by every model
(Table~\ref{tab:byclass}): \emph{Lean~Right} is the hardest label for
all four (exact accuracy $0.07$--$0.46$; on average $2.85$ of $4$ models
wrong per article, against under $1.0$ on the Left and Right extremes),
and difficulty falls as an article's expert lean strengthens (Spearman
$\rho{=}-0.37$, $p{<}10^{-7}$)---the models read the poles well and lose
the moderate right. Crucially, the misreads are \emph{directional}:
misclassified Lean-Right articles are pulled \emph{left}, not merely
blurred to center---$33\%$ are labeled Lean-Left or Left and $21\%$
Center, while Center articles are called Lean-Left $32\%$ of the time
versus Lean-Right $4\%$. A ``balanced feed'' or lean-labeling product
built on these classifications therefore \emph{systematically
under-counts right-leaning content}. The pattern is not one model's
quirk: it holds across both vendors and both roles, consistent with
concurrent reports of ``center-collapse'' in frontier-model news
classification \citep{kennedy2026left}, which our per-class
decomposition refines into a right-to-left asymmetry.

Two riders help a builder localize the risk. By \emph{topic}, lean
classification is least reliable on policy-abstract coverage
(elections/governance, economy, climate: $1.9$ of $4$ models wrong) and
most reliable on identity-concrete coverage (social/culture,
crime/justice: $\approx1.1$). And the models' \emph{confidence} is
diagnostic but must be recalibrated before display: they are markedly
overconfident (mean stated confidence $0.88$ vs.\ actual accuracy
$0.60$), and only the top bin is trustworthy ($87\%$ accurate at
confidence ${\ge}0.95$, near chance below $0.90$; confidence--correctness
AUC $0.75$). Confidence is a usable \emph{ordering}, not a usable
probability.

\section{Deployable triage}
The map reduces to a short display-and-routing policy.
\begin{itemize}
  \item \emph{Triage by agreement, not confidence.} Route the articles
  where independent models disagree (or either is under $0.85$
  confident) to human review; auto-clear the confident-agreement
  cases. On our data this clears $43\%$ of volume at $85\%$ accuracy
  while catching $86\%$ of all errors---and beats a confidence
  threshold at the same routing volume. Do not expose raw confidence as
  a probability.
  \item \emph{Show word-anchored flags; hide inferential ones.} Surface
  loaded-word, opinion-as-fact, and negativity flags; present omission,
  framing, and unsupported-claim flags---and individual highlights---only
  where independent models agree.
  \item \emph{Correct for the right-ward blind spot.} Expect
  moderate-right articles to be under-labeled; a product aiming at
  balance should treat a model's ``center'' label on right-of-center
  content as a routing trigger, not a verdict.
  \item \emph{Re-audit every model upgrade.} The reliability map is
  model-specific; the full evaluation re-runs on a new model for
  $\sim$\$150 in API cost---a regression test, not a research project.
\end{itemize}

\section{Discussion and Limitations}
These results are a reliability map, not a bias verdict: they tell a
builder which parts of a frontier-model bias detector today's models can
power and which still need cross-model agreement, filters, or humans.
Two limits bound the claims. All findings are \emph{exploratory} (not
pre-registered) and computed on the baseline task condition; they are
sized for design guidance, not confirmatory inference. And every
accuracy statistic inherits AllSides' outlet-anchored labels as ground
truth \citep{baly2020detect}, so the directional error could partly
reflect article-vs-outlet label drift---though uniform label noise
cannot produce a one-sided right-to-left pattern. Finally, any LLM judge
inside a tool's own quality-control pipeline inherits the reliability
problem we document for the models themselves, and needs the
pre-committed validation the companion paper specifies.
